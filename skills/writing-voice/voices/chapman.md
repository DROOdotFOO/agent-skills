# David Chapman

Source: https://meaningness.com
Studied from: "About my writing", "Geeks MOPs and sociopaths in subculture evolution", "A first lesson in meta-rationality", "A bridge to meta-rationality vs. civilizational collapse", "Nebulosity", various Meaningness book chapters

## The core move

Chapman thinks out loud on the page -- you watch him arrive at conclusions rather than receive pre-packaged ones. The prose has visible cognitive motion.

## Sentence rhythm

- Varied, with a bias toward medium-length sentences punctuated by short declarative ones
- Builds through a paragraph with increasingly complex sentences, then drops a short one for emphasis
- Comfortable with long comma-heavy clauses: "Many of the people I care about most, and find most interesting, are STEM-educated refugees from ideological rationalism."
- Uses em dashes for mid-sentence direction changes -- the thought literally turns in front of you
- Will write a one-sentence paragraph when the idea earns it

## Paragraph structure

- Ranges from 1 sentence to 6-7 sentences -- dramatically varied
- Often opens paragraphs mid-thought, as if continuing a conversation you walked into
- Closes paragraphs by arriving somewhere unexpected -- not summarizing, but landing on a new question or a surprising restatement
- Single-sentence paragraphs appear roughly every 4-5 paragraphs, usually for a pivot or a punchline

## Rhetorical moves

1. **Thinking-in-progress**: Sentences where you can see the idea developing. "They've mastered rationality, they've seen through it -- and many now are stuck." The em dash is him realizing the conclusion as he writes it.

2. **Generous definitions**: Pauses to define terms not as jargon setup but as gifts that reveal his values. "I'm using 'geek' here to mean 'someone fascinated by the details of a subject most people don't care about.'" The definition is more interesting than the term.

3. **Productive digression**: Connects things that don't obviously belong together. An essay about subcultures references The Office's Gervais Principle. An essay about civilizational collapse references Gandalf and the Balrog. The digression isn't decoration -- it's where the actual insight lives.

4. **Visible ambivalence**: "I am, somewhat reluctantly, a Buddhist. Of an odd sort." He'll tell you he doesn't fully buy his own position. This isn't hedging -- it's intellectual honesty that makes the claims he does commit to land harder.

5. **Parenthetical self-awareness**: Uses parentheticals for wry commentary on his own argument. "(Ignore the distracting skeuomorphic ring-binder graphic in the middle; it's not part of the problem.)" Light, human, never self-important.

6. **The explanation IS the thinking**: When he explains something to you, the explanation itself is where the interesting cognitive work happens. He doesn't explain as a detour before getting to the point -- the explanation is the point.

7. **Questions as genuine uncertainty**: "What -- if anything -- is 'purpose'?" Not rhetorical setup. He's actually asking, and the essay is him working through possible answers in front of you.

## Emotional register

- Uncertainty is displayed openly, not as weakness but as intellectual seriousness. If he doesn't know, he says so, and that admission becomes load-bearing.
- Strong claims are earned slowly. He'll spend 3-4 paragraphs turning an idea over before committing to a position. When he does commit, the reader trusts it because they watched the deliberation.
- Humor is dry, parenthetical, self-aware. Never performative. Often the funniest line is a throwaway aside.
- Being wrong or not knowing is treated as interesting rather than embarrassing. "We are always already walking on clouds, because there is no ground anywhere" -- that's not comfort, it's wonder.

## What to borrow

1. **Sit with an idea before moving on.** When you've said something interesting, spend 2-3 more sentences exploring it from a different angle instead of immediately advancing the argument. Let the reader feel the weight of the idea.

2. **Add one productive digression per essay.** Connect your subject to something unexpected -- not as metaphor but as structural parallel. The digression should surprise the reader and then make the main argument clearer.

3. **Show one moment of genuine uncertainty.** Not a risk register item, not a caveat -- an actual "I don't know." Written as a person sitting with the feeling of not knowing, not as a lawyer covering bases.

4. **Vary paragraph length dramatically.** A single-sentence paragraph followed by a 6-sentence paragraph that explores one idea from three angles. The rhythm variation signals a thinking person, not an information delivery system.

5. **Define one thing for an outsider.** Pick a term your audience knows and explain it as if to a smart stranger. The explanation will reveal something interesting about the concept that even insiders haven't articulated.

## What NOT to borrow

- **Over-qualifying everything.** Chapman sometimes hedges so much that the claim dissolves. In technical writing this becomes mush. When you know something, say it.
- **Abstract nouns as protagonists.** "Nebulosity", "meaningness", "meta-rationality" -- these work in his philosophical context but in technical or product writing they read as vague. Keep concrete nouns as subjects.
- **The slow build for its own sake.** Chapman can spend 2000 words before arriving at a thesis. In a blog post about software, this patience becomes frustrating. Borrow the _moments_ of slowness, not the overall pace.
- **Shared cultural assumptions about his audience.** Chapman writes for "STEM-educated refugees from ideological rationalism" -- a specific slice of people. References that assume this background won't land universally.
- **Lack of specificity.** "Cultural and community context for the 4-5 transition has, thus far, been rare." Rare how? Where? Chapman can be frustratingly vague when a concrete example would anchor the point. Your writing is already better at this -- don't lose that.

## Calibration anchors

### Anchor 1: Visible cognitive motion

> Many of the people I care about most, and find most interesting, are STEM-educated refugees from ideological rationalism. They've mastered rationality, they've seen through it -- and many now are stuck.

### Anchor 2: Generous definition with values

> I'm using 'geek' here to mean 'someone fascinated by the details of a subject most people don't care about.'

### Anchor 3: Ambivalence as strength

> I am, somewhat reluctantly, a Buddhist. Of an odd sort: 'the opposite of whatever you'd expect' comes close.

### Anchor 4: Philosophical comfort with groundlessness

> We are always already walking on clouds, because there is no ground anywhere.

### Anchor 5: Productive connection of distant ideas

> This is about the American ruling class. It is about postmodernism, and Gandalf's showdown with the Balrog at Khazad-Dûm, and the fall of Sam Bankman-Fried.
